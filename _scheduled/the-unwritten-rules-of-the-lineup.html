The Unwritten Rules of the Lineup

Surfing has no rulebook. It has something worse — everyone knows the rules and nobody wrote them down. A guide to the code that runs the water.

If you show up to a surf break for the first time and ask someone to explain the rules, they'll look at you the way people look at someone who asks what fork to use at a dinner party. Faint disbelief. A quick assessment of whether you're serious. Then a vague gesture in the direction of the water.

The rules are not explained. They are absorbed over time, through observation and the occasional incident. Breaking them is how most people find out they exist.

What the lineup actually is

The lineup is where surfers wait for waves. It sits beyond the breaking point, usually far enough out that the white water doesn't reach you. You paddle out, you find where everyone else is sitting, and you join the queue.

That word — queue — is where it gets complicated. The lineup is a queue that moves, reforms, has no visible markings, and is managed entirely through a social contract nobody signed. It works because breaking the code makes the session worse for everyone, including yourself.

The ocean doesn't care who you are. The locals might.

The rules nobody told you

Don't drop in

The surfer closest to the peak has right of way. Always. If someone is already up and riding and you take off on the same wave in front of them, you've dropped in. This is the one that matters. The equivalent, on land, of cutting in front of someone at the bar who's been waiting longer, taking their drink, and acting confused when they're annoyed.

New surfers do it because they didn't see the other person. Experienced surfers do it occasionally on purpose. Neither version goes unnoticed.

Don't snake

Repeatedly paddling around other surfers to get closer to the peak — jumping the queue over and over. It looks like positioning. Everyone in the water can see it. The person being snaked can always tell.

Respect the locals

People who surf a break regularly know how the wave breaks, where to sit, what the tide does. They've earned some priority through time. Most breaks open up quickly to people who show up without an agenda. Most don't open up to people who treat the place like it exists for them.

Nobody agreed on the rules. Everyone knows when you break them.

After the session

The drop-in, the snake, the argument about whose wave that was — almost all of it gets forgotten in the car park. Someone mentions the set that came through at nine. Someone describes the wave that nobody thought was makeable. The wipeout gets slightly worse with each retelling.

The rules are for the water. After that it's just people who got up early, talking about it.

Route 66: The Road That Refused to Die

They decommissioned it in 1985. Nobody told the road. Why the Mother Road is more alive now than it has been in decades — and what that says about how we want to travel.

In 1985, the US Highway System officially removed Route 66 from its maps. The interstate network had made it redundant. The Mother Road, they said, had served its purpose.

Forty years later, over 1.4 million international tourists visit sections of it every year. Local shops along the route report that foreign visitors outnumber Americans five to one. The centennial is 2026, and the interest is already building.

The road they decommissioned is, by most measures, the most romanticised stretch of asphalt on the planet.

You can't kill a road that people need to exist.

What Route 66 actually is

The official version: 2,448 miles of highway running from Chicago to Santa Monica, commissioned in 1926, crossing eight states, officially decommissioned in 1985. The actual version: a two-lane window into what America looked like before everything got faster.

The diners that refused to update their menus. The motels with neon signs humming in the desert dark. The roadside attractions so gloriously ridiculous — ten Cadillacs nose-down in a Texas field, a whale the size of a house in the middle of Oklahoma — that you pull over not because you planned to but because you simply have to.

Route 66 was never really about getting somewhere. It was always about the getting there. The interstate replaced it precisely because people wanted to move faster. What's interesting is that the people seeking it out now are coming specifically because it's slower.

Why it's back

Route 66 rewards slow travel. You can drive 100 miles one day and 300 the next. There's no itinerary unless you make one. The best moments, by most accounts, are the ones you didn't plan — a conversation with a museum volunteer in Kansas, a pie at a diner in New Mexico that turns a twenty-minute stop into two hours.

People seeking it out now are coming specifically because it's slower. The interstate replaced it precisely because people wanted to move faster. Forty years later, the slower road is the one with the traffic.

The centennial in 2026 is generating real momentum. Preservation projects have re-lit neon signs that went dark decades ago. A new Route 66 Experience opened in Tulsa. There are plans to designate it a National Historic Trail. The road is being looked after in a way it hasn't been for fifty years.

The pint of view from the passenger seat

The destination is never really the point. The Grand Canyon is at one end of Route 66's most dramatic stretch, genuinely astonishing when you get there. But the people who've driven it will tell you the moments that stick are almost always somewhere in the middle. A conversation. A view. A cold beer at a roadside bar where everyone in the room is a stranger and nobody stays that way for long.

The journey is the excuse. The people you end up talking to are the point.

★

Route 66 turns 100 next year. The neon is back on. The diners are open. The road still goes all the way to the Pacific.

For the ones who take the long way.
